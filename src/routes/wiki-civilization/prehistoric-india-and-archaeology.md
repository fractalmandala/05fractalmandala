The Indian subcontinent's prehistoric record stretches from hominin activity in the Siwalik Hills over two million years ago to the decline of the Sindhu-Sarasvati urban complex around 1900 BCE — a trajectory that demonstrates not a passive reception of western influences but a primary theater of indigenous development. The religious implications are profound: fire worship, Mother Goddess veneration, aniconic linga-like stones, the pipal tree cult, and elaborate funerary practices with proto-samsaric assumptions all predate the Vedic textual record by millennia. The archaeological data inverts the conventional story of Indian religion as an arrival from outside, revealing instead a deep indigenous symbolic substrate upon which later Vedic, Harappan, and classical formations were layered.

### *Base Archaeological Data* (archaeology-base.md)

This survey covers the prehistoric trajectory from the Lower Palaeolithic to the Neolithic threshold.

**Lower Palaeolithic — the Siwalik Horizon:** The Masol site in the Siwalik Frontal Range (paleomagnetically dated to over 2 million years ago) has yielded bones with anthropic cut marks — possibly the oldest evidence of hominin behavior in Asia. The Riwat site in Pakistan contains Pre-Acheulian stone tools. At Attirampakkam in Tamil Nadu, Acheulian handaxe sites date to nearly a million years earlier than previously hypothesized, showing the Deccan Peninsula was occupied by tool-making hominins far earlier than the standard model assumed. The Narmada calvarium (Hathnora, 1982) — "Narmada Man" — is a Middle-to-Late Pleistocene specimen displaying a mosaic of *Homo erectus* and archaic *Homo sapiens* features, its classification still disputed.

**Middle and Upper Palaeolithic — the Toba event:** At Jwalapuram (Andhra Pradesh), stone tools appear both above and below the volcanic ash from the Toba super-eruption (~74,000 years ago), demonstrating that human populations survived the catastrophe with minimal cultural disruption. The Upper Palaeolithic brings blade and burin industries, and crucially, the first evidence of symbolic behavior: ostrich eggshell beads (dating back ~30,000 years) at Patne and Bhimbetka, engraved with geometric designs that are the earliest manufactured ornaments in South Asia.

**Mesolithic — microlithic technology and Bhimbetka:** Microliths (geometric flake tools, 1–5 cm) hafted into composite weapons define the Mesolithic toolkit. Sites in the Ganga plain (Sarai Nahar Rai, Mahadaha, Damdama) have produced the largest collection of Mesolithic human skeletons in Asia — exceptionally robust, taller than contemporary European Mesolithic populations, with musculoskeletal stress markers consistent with a mobile hunting lifestyle. Burials were intentional, structured, and east-oriented. The Bhimbetka rock shelters contain paintings depicting hunting scenes, communal dances, symbolic animals, and possible conflict — the earliest evidence of shared visual culture and spiritual imagination in the subcontinent. First evidence of domesticated animals (sheep, goats) appears at Adamgarh and Bagor around 5000–2800 BCE.

**Neolithic — multiple regional traditions:** Rather than a single agricultural revolution, India displays several distinct Neolithic clusters: (1) **Mehrgarh** in Balochistan (~7000 BCE onward) — the most thoroughly documented transition from semi-nomadic cultivation to full urbanism, with wheat/barley cultivation, mud-brick multi-roomed houses, graves with lapis lazuli from Afghanistan and turquoise from Iran (evidence of long-distance trade from the very beginning), and by Period II/III, wheel-made ceramics and copper metalworking. (2) **Belan Valley** (Koldihwa, Mahagara) — early rice cultivation, a planned village layout with a cattle pen identified by preserved hoof marks in clay. (3) **Kashmir** (Burzahom, Gufkral) — subterranean pit-dwellings adapted to harsh winters, burials with dogs and antler horns. (4) **South Indian ashmounds** (Deccan Plateau) — massive accumulations of burnt cow dung, interpreted as sites of seasonal pastoral ritual, communal exchange, or both, associated with cattle-centric religion that prefigures the later sacralization of the cow.

### *Sacred Foundations: Divinity in Ancient India from Prehistory to 500 BCE* (res-divinity.md)

This research document traces the archaeological evidence for religious thought and divine conceptualization from the Upper Palaeolithic to the dawn of the historical era, with particular focus on the Sindhu-Sarasvati Civilization (SSC) and its continuities with later Hindu practice.

**Baghor I — the oldest shrine in South Asia (~9000–8000 BCE):** In the Son River Valley (Madhya Pradesh), a natural triangular piece of sandstone placed on a circular rubble platform was identified as a deliberate ritual installation — morphologically similar to the Kali Yantra (geometric representation of the Mother Goddess). Local tribal groups (Kol, Baiga) in the same valley continue to worship similar triangular stones as "Mai" (the Mother). The ritual practice has endured for over 11,000 years, representing one of the most profound instances of cultural continuity in human history.

**Rock art and distributed sacred space:** Mesolithic rock paintings across Central India (Madhya Pradesh, Uttar Pradesh, Rajasthan) depict community dances, hunting scenes, and — critically in the Neolithic layers — tridents and yogic figures. Recurring pigments (red ochre, white) persist as ritual colors into modern Hindu and tribal festivals. The distribution of ritual sites across the landscape, rather than concentration in a single center, matches the networked logic of sacred geography analyzed by Eck (see [[indian-sacred-geography]]).

**Sindhu-Sarasvati Civilization (SSC) — the Sarasvati bed sites:**

- **Bhirrana** (Haryana, from ~7570 BCE): Oldest known SSC site; early kilns reaching 900–1000°C; a potsherd bearing a graffiti replica of the "dancing girl" from Mohenjo-daro, suggesting this iconic figure was a shared symbolic type across the entire basin.
- **Kunal**: Silver crowns and regalia — evidence of a ritual or social elite, a prerequisite for organized priesthood.
- **Sothi-Siswal** (~4600–3200 BCE): Pipal (*Ficus religiosa*) leaf motifs on ceramics — the same tree revered in modern Hinduism and Buddhism, marking one of the clearest continuities between SSC and later traditions.
- **Rakhigarhi** (Haryana): Largest SSC city (~350 hectares). Fire altars and sacrificial chambers on citadel podiums containing charred bone — definitive evidence of fire-centric ritual. The burial ground (RGR-7) shows three concurrent burial types (primary, secondary, symbolic cenotaphs), indicating diverse eschatological beliefs coexisting in the same community.
- **Kalibangan** (Rajasthan): The most detailed map of SSC ritual space. Fire altars in three distinct spatial contexts: (1) communal citadel altars on raised platforms, (2) domestic fire-pits in private courtyards, (3) isolated altars for specialized rituals. A well and bathing area adjacent to the altars suggest ritual purification (*abhiṣeka*, *snāna*) was a prerequisite — directly paralleling later Vedic and Purāṇic practice. Notably, Kalibangan is almost entirely devoid of Mother Goddess figurines (ubiquitous at Mohenjo-daro and Harappa), suggesting a distinct religious identity — fire/water/atmospheric rather than fertility-centric.

**The Pashupati seal debate:** The famous seal from Mohenjo-daro depicts a seated figure in yogic posture, wearing a horned headdress, surrounded by an elephant, tiger, rhinoceros, buffalo, and two deer. Interpretations range from Proto-Śiva/Paśupati (John Marshall — yogic posture, tricephalic, lord of beasts) to Vedic Agni (three heads = three forms of fire) to a female goddess (Herbert Sullivan — jewelry and hairstyle read as female). The debate remains unresolved, but the presence of linga-shaped stones at Harappa and Kalibangan in ritual contexts suggests that aniconic phallic symbolism was already emerging alongside the Mother Goddess tradition — the exact combination that defines classical Śaiva religion.

**Post-urban transition — Sinauli (~1900–1800 BCE):** The Sinauli excavations revealed a militarized elite using coffin burials with copper-decorated wooden chariots, antenna swords, helmets, and ritual copper utensils. The warrior-priest complex visible here provides the sociological substrate for what the Upanishads would later call "the secret of the Kṣatriyas" — rebirth as a warrior doctrine for overcoming the fear of death. Sinauli challenges both the "peaceful Harappan" model and the late-dating of chariot culture in South Asia.
