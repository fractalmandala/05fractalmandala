---
title: vedic deities and mythology
id: 19
---

The Indian pantheon did not arrive fully formed: it developed through a collision between the patriarchal, sky-oriented Indo-European deities (the devas, "bright ones") and the chthonic, fertility-centered, matriarchal traditions of the subcontinent's pre-Aryan populations. Over roughly two millennia — from the Ṛgveda through the early Purāṇas — hundreds of distinct gods consolidated, through competition, absorption, and attrition, into the epic-Purāṇic triad of Brahman, Viṣṇu, and Śiva. The deities that survived did so by absorbing regional cults, expanding their functional portfolios, and becoming objects of devotional worship (pūjā) rather than merely of sacrificial ritual.

## Sources

### Dandekar, *Vedic Mythological Tracts*

Dandekar's essays apply what he calls "evolutionary mythology" to the Vedic corpus: Vedic texts are palimpsests, layered with strata from different periods and populations, and individual hymns often preserve contradictory characterizations of the same deity as older and newer conceptions coexist.

**Savitr** — The solar deity of impulsion and forward movement; associated with the golden hands that set all things in motion. Distinct from Sūrya (the sun as seen) — Savitr is the sun's animating power.

**Varuṇa** — In the earlier Ṛgveda, Varuṇa is an *asura* (lord of power) in the positive sense — omniscient cosmic sovereign who guards ṛta (cosmic order) through his network of *spasas* (divine spies) and enforces it with his noose (*pāśa*). He is approached with guilt, confession, and prayer for forgiveness. Dandekar reads the *asura* theology as a remnant of a pre-Aryan divine complex later demonized when the Indra-fighting tradition displaced the old sovereign gods.

**Viṣṇu** — In the Ṛgveda, a relatively minor deity notable for his "three strides" (which later tradition reads as the three cosmic levels). Dandekar traces how Viṣṇu accumulated solar traits and then, through identification with the Vedic sacrificial pillar (yūpa) and eventually with Vāsudeva-Kṛṣṇa, became the center of devotional theology.

**Indra** — The greatest warrior-god of the Ṛgveda, slayer of Vṛtra (the dragon blocking the cosmic waters). Dandekar raises the possibility that Indra was originally a human hero elevated to divine status — the Vedic tradition's practice of deifying exceptional warriors. His dominance in the Ṛgveda later fades as Viṣṇu and Śiva absorb his functions.

**Rudra** — Non-Aryan in origin, propitiated rather than celebrated, addressed with anxiety. His epithet Śiva ("auspicious") is apotropaic — you call him auspicious so he doesn't harm you. Dandekar reads Rudra as the pre-Aryan deity most successfully absorbed into the Vedic canon, and subsequently the one who overwhelms it.

**Agni** — The fire-god, present at every sacrifice as the mediating principle between human and divine. The most extensively praised deity in the Ṛgveda in terms of hymn count. Dandekar traces how Agni's crematory fire function later flows into Śiva, who becomes identified with the cremation ground and its fires.

**Yama** — God of the dead (not of death itself); in the Ṛgveda, a benevolent figure who discovered the path to death and now presides over the ancestors. Associated with the Indo-European creator-twin mythology (cf. Yima/Iranian, Ymir/Norse). Later absorbed Mṛtyu (death), Kāla (time), and Dharma (cosmic righteousness) to become Dharmarāja, the impartial cosmic judge.

**Vasiṣṭha** — The paradigmatic brahmin seer, in perpetual rivalry with Vishvamitra. Hymns of Book 7 bear his name and reflect his vision of cosmic order and priestly authority.

### Bhattacharji, *Indian Theogony*

Bhattacharji's *Indian Theogony* is the first sustained historical study in English of how the Indian pantheon developed across textual strata from the Ṛgveda to the early Purāṇas. Her method: track historical diffusion first; invoke structural archetypes only when diffusion explanations are exhausted. She draws on Eliade's insistence that myths project vital collective experiences, and Lévi-Strauss's structural approach.

**Three strata model:** (1) *Indo-European stratum* — Dyaus (=Ouranos=Tiwaz), Varuṇa (=Ahura Mazda), Mitra (=Iranian Mithra), Aśvins (=Dioscuri); (2) *Vedic-Brahmanical stratum* — modification through contact with Austric, Dravidian, and Munda populations; non-Aryan wives brought their deities into the household (Gṛhya) rites; (3) *Epic-Purāṇic stratum* — under Buddhist/Jain pressure and the Gupta consolidation, Brahmanism reinvented itself through the Purāṇas, producing the triad, the avatāra doctrine, and "neo-Brahmanism."

**The Aryan vs. non-Aryan structural tension:** The Aryans brought a patriarchal, solar, sky-oriented, sacrificial complex (the Yajña, the Nigamic tradition). What they found in India was a population worshipping Pitṛs (ancestors), earth-goddesses, fertility deities, Yakṣas, and Nāgas — a matriarchal/lunar/chthonic complex (the Āgamic tradition). The Indian pantheon is the product of this collision. Viṣṇu represents solar-agrarian ripening from above; Śiva and Yama represent the dark processes beneath the soil.

**Varuṇa's decline:** Varuṇa's all-seeing nature, his *asura* epithet, and his nocturnal moral severity made him a candidate for demonization. His functions were distributed: omniscient moral authority went to the developing Dharmarāja-Yama complex; dark associations and the noose went to Śiva.

**Yama's multiplication:** In the Mahābhārata, Yama appears as four distinct figures in a single narrative (Yama, Kāla, Antaka, Mṛtyu) — illustrating how one divine complex absorbs multiple conceptual layers. The Naciketas episode in the Kaṭhopaniṣad presents Yama as the ultimate teacher of metaphysics: the god of the dead becomes the teacher of what lies beyond death.

**Śiva's absorption mechanism:** Śiva's rise to supremacy required absorbing Skanda (war-god), Gaṇapati (elephant-god, made his son), Kubera (wealth, made his friend), Agni (crematory fire), Puṣan (cattle-deity), and portions of Indra's warrior portfolio. The Dakṣa-sacrifice episode — where Śiva is excluded from the Vedic sacrifice by the orthodox Dakṣa, Satī kills herself, and Śiva destroys the sacrifice — is read by Bhattacharji as mythologized social history: the moment when Śiva's cult overwhelmed Vedic sacrificial orthodoxy.

**The Mother Goddess composite:** Śiva's consort is not one goddess but a layered composite — Umā, Aditi, Lakṣmī, Uṣas, Sarasvatī, and the mountain-goddess traditions of non-Aryan populations all feed into Pārvatī-Durgā-Kālī. The Ardhanārīśvara concept (Śiva and Pārvatī fused in a single half-male, half-female body) represents the philosophical resolution of the Aryan-non-Aryan polarity.

**Viṣṇu group:** Viṣṇu's solar legacy (the ripening sun, the three strides traversing the cosmos) positioned him as the agrarian deity par excellence. His absorption of Vāsudeva-Kṛṣṇa worship — supported by the Boghaz-keui inscription evidence for Vedic gods in the 14th century BCE Mitanni treaty — gave him the devotional (bhakti) following that made him one of the two great sectarian poles. The avatāra doctrine — Viṣṇu descending into the world in successive forms to restore dharma — is traced as a mechanism for absorbing local hero-cults (Rāma, Kṛṣṇa, Paraśurāma) into the Vaiṣṇava umbrella.
