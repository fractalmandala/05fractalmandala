India is not a political or ethnic unit that happens to have religious sites within it; it is constituted as a meaningful whole through a network of sacred places, pilgrimage routes, and mythological connections that link its diverse regions into a continuously enacted landscape. No single center organizes this landscape hierarchically; instead, sacred presence distributes itself through repetition and replication — the same deity, the same river, the same pattern of sacred significance appears again and again across the subcontinent, each instance confirming rather than diluting the original. Diana Eck's *India: A Sacred Geography* is the foundational study of this structure.

## Sources

### Eck, *India: A Sacred Geography*

**The problem of Banaras:** Eck opens with what appears to be the most obviously "central" site in Hindu India — Banaras (Vārāṇasī/Kāśī), the city where death grants liberation. But placing Banaras alongside parallel claims made for Ayodhya, Mathura, Hardwar, Ujjain, Kanchipuram, and Dwarka — each described within the same tradition as *mokṣadāyaka* (givers of liberation) — reveals that uniqueness cannot be sustained. There is not one center but many. The category of "center" itself becomes unstable.

**From center to network:** The landscape of India is marked not by the prominence of one site over others but by the repeated appearance of the same forms across different locations. There are "other Kāśīs" — the "Kāśī of the South," the "Hidden Kāśī" in the Himalayas — each reproducing the symbolic structure of the original. Temples bearing the name of Viśvanātha appear far beyond Banaras. Specific ritual sites (like Maṇikarṇikā ghāṭ) find counterparts in distant regions. This is not imitation or dilution; it is a different logic of sacred presence, in which replication is the extension of meaning rather than its diminishment.

**The logic of repetition:** Sacred geography is organized through patterned distribution: the seven sacred rivers, the twelve jyotirlingas (Śiva's self-manifestations as pillars of light), the fifty-one Śakti pīṭhas (goddess sites where Satī's body fell). These are not exhaustive literal inventories; they express a principle by which sacred presence avoids confinement to a single point. Śiva is not limited to twelve locations; the jyotirlingas articulate a pattern through which his presence is made accessible across the subcontinent. Repetition is the means by which the sacred becomes pervasive rather than concentrated.

**India as an imagined landscape:** Eck adopts Benedict Anderson's "imagined community" framework but applies it to sacred geography. The connections between India's sacred sites are not physically visible; they exist in stories, ritual associations, shared recognitions, and the cumulative memory of pilgrims. A pilgrim approaching a local temple encounters it not as an isolated location but as part of a network mapped in narrative and memory — other places' names appear within the ritual geography of local ones; myths tie together regions separated by vast distances. This network is "imagined" in the sense that it depends on acts of interpretation and participation, not in the sense that it is fictive.

**Tīrtha — crossing and connection:** The word *tīrtha* means "ford" — a crossing place in a river. In religious usage, it becomes a place where the boundary between human and divine is permeable: a site of "crossing over." But Eck extends the meaning: a tīrtha is not only a vertical crossing between worlds but a horizontal connection within the world. Each tīrtha exists within a network of other tīrthas, linked through narrative, ritual, and pilgrimage routes. Sacred geography is structured through a multiplicity of these nodes, each participating in a network that extends across the subcontinent.

**Pilgrimage as the practice of connection:** Pilgrimage (*tīrthayātrā*) is not merely one religious practice among others; it is the primary means by which the sacred geography is continuously enacted. Pilgrims trace and reinforce the connections between sites. Over generations, the repeated movement of pilgrims creates what Eck calls "connective tissue" — paths, sequences, circuits that make the landscape intelligible as a whole. The Mahābhārata explicitly equates the merit of pilgrimage with that of sacrifice, representing a democratic expansion of religious access: where sacrifice required resources and priestly mediation, pilgrimage requires only the willingness to move.

**What is India?:** Eck reframes the question from "what is India?" (a definition problem) to "how is India held together?" (a relational problem). India's unity is not territorial (a bounded space), linguistic (a single language), or politically continuous. It is relational, produced through the linking of places across distance by shared practices, stories, and recognitions. India is a landscape that becomes coherent through the accumulation of these connections — not given, but continuously made and remade.
