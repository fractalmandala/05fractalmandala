For Eliade, the sacred and the profane are not two categories of things but two modes of being in the world — two existential orientations. The person who experiences the sacred inhabits a different universe from the person who does not: they live in a cosmos structured around a center (axis mundi), periodically renewed by a sacred time that abolishes ordinary historical time and returns to the primordial moment of creation. The terror of purely secular, historical consciousness — which cannot find in the flux of events any meaning, any pattern, any redemption — is Eliade's implicit diagnosis of modernity.

---

## Sources

### Eliade, *The Sacred and the Profane*

Eliade opens with the distinction between hierophany (the manifestation of the sacred in something) and the profane (ordinary, undifferentiated existence). Any object can become a hierophany — a stone, a tree, a spring — not because it ceases to be what it is, but because it reveals something beyond itself. The sacred does not abolish the natural; it transfigures it by manifesting through it.

**Sacred space:** Profane space is homogeneous and relative — no point has any privileged quality. Sacred space is heterogeneous and absolute: there is a fixed point, a center, from which the rest of the world derives its orientation and meaning. This center (axis mundi) takes various forms — the cosmic mountain, the World Tree, the temple pillar, the sacred city — but its function is constant: it is the point where the three cosmic zones (sky, earth, underworld) communicate, where the human can pass from one to another.

The threshold is sacred — the doorstep, the city gate, the altar boundary — because it marks the transition from profane to sacred space. A house is not merely a dwelling; it is an image of the cosmos, with its hearth as the center-point and its orientation replicating cosmic directions. To build a house or found a city is to repeat the divine act of cosmogony — establishing order in chaos.

**Sacred time:** Ordinary time is linear, historical, cumulative — time in which things are worn down, used up, forgotten. Sacred time is circular and reversible: the ritual returns the celebrant to the primordial moment of creation (in illo tempore — "in that time"). This is not a fiction or a metaphor; for the religious person, the ritual genuinely participates in the sacred time of origins. Festivals do not commemorate past events; they reenact them — the event becomes present.

**Nature as hierophany:** The sky, the sun, the moon, the earth, the waters — each reveals something about the sacred through its structure. The sky's transcendence and immensity become metaphors for divine transcendence; the moon's cycle of waxing and waning makes it the paradigmatic image of death and rebirth. Nature is not inert background but a field of symbolic disclosure.

**Desacralization of modernity:** Eliade's implicit argument is that modern secular humanity has not transcended religious consciousness but has impoverished itself by abandoning it — without sacred time, all time is homogeneous flux; without sacred space, all locations are equivalent; without cosmic orientation, existence loses its structure. This is the "terror of history" treated more fully in his companion work.

### Eliade, *The Myth of the Eternal Return*

This earlier and more philosophical work develops the argument implicit in *The Sacred and the Profane*: that archaic humanity structured its existence around the deliberate abolition of profane historical time through return to the sacred time of origins.

**Archetypes and repetition:** No act is real unless it imitates or repeats an archetype — an act performed by a god or hero in illo tempore. The farmer who plants crops does not merely perform an agricultural operation; he repeats the cosmogonic act of divine planting. The warrior does not merely fight an enemy; he reenacts the god's defeat of the dragon of chaos. Without this connection to the archetype, the act is merely secular — real but not fully real, effective but not cosmically significant.

**The abolition of history:** The festival, the ritual, the myth all serve the same function: to annihilate ordinary temporal duration and restore the sacred time of origins. What archaic humanity could not tolerate was purely linear, irreversible, meaningless time — time that merely accumulates without leading anywhere. The ritual cycle offers escape from this "terror of history" by periodically returning everything to the beginning.

**Only the sacred is fully real:** This ontological principle underlies all of Eliade's work. The profane is not unreal, but it is less real — less dense, less structured, less meaningful — than the sacred. The archetype is more real than its earthly instances. The myth is more real than the historical event it interprets.

**The irruption of historical consciousness:** The Hebrew prophets introduced a genuinely new element: God acts in history, not in a mythological time of origins; salvation is accomplished once, not cyclically repeated. Christianity inherited this historical orientation and intensified it. The result is the modern sense of time as linear, irreversible, and unrepeatable — which generates both the achievements of historical consciousness (progress, development, unique events) and its burdens (meaningless suffering, entropy, death without redemption).

### Eck, *India: A Sacred Geography* (sacred space dimension)

Eck's work on India's sacred landscape is treated in full at [[indian-sacred-geography]]. Its contribution to this topic: Eck demonstrates that Indian sacred space does not conform to Eliade's model of a single sacred center radiating outward. Instead, India presents a polycentric, networked sacred landscape in which centers are multiplied, reproduced, and distributed — a logic of repetition rather than hierarchy. The sacred is present everywhere because it keeps appearing again, not because it radiates from one point. This challenges and complicates Eliade's model of the axis mundi without displacing the basic insight that sacred space is heterogeneous.
