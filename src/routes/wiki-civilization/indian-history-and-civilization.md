D.D. Kosambi approached Indian history with tools borrowed from Marx and from field archaeology: the mode of production (how a society organizes the extraction of surplus from nature and from other humans) determines the forms that religion, law, and culture take. This materialist methodology did not make him reductive — his two major works are full of close attention to textual detail, myth, and ritual — but it gave him a framework for explaining why things change, not just describing that they do. His work remains the most consequential attempt to situate Indian cultural and religious history within a theory of social formation.

## Sources

### Kosambi, *Myth and Reality*

*Myth and Reality* (1962) collects five essays on Indian culture approached through the combination of fieldwork and textual evidence that Kosambi called "the method of survivals" — identifying pre-historical social formations preserved in contemporary folk practice or textual anomaly.

**The Bhagavad Gīta and class function:** Kosambi's most famous essay reads the Bhagavad Gīta not as timeless spiritual wisdom but as a text produced at a specific historical moment — the consolidation of a surplus-extracting warrior-brahmin alliance over an agrarian population. The Gīta's teaching of niṣkāma karma (action without attachment to results) functions ideologically to legitimize the performance of one's social duty (svadharma) — which, for the warrior, means killing — without guilt. Kosambi dates the composition to the period of the Maurya-to-Gupta transition, when the warrior class needed a philosophical legitimation of its function.

**The Urvashi myth:** The Urvashi-Purūravas myth (RV 10.95; later in the Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa and dramas) encodes a transitional moment in Indian social history: the shift from matrilineal to patrilineal kinship organization. Urvashi, the apsarā (celestial nymph) who temporarily takes a human husband, represents the earlier matrilineal order; her departure represents the male heir's need for patrilineal legitimacy. The myth "remembers" a social transition that historical records do not preserve.

**Goddess of the Crossroads:** Kosambi's fieldwork in Maharashtra identified a widespread goddess cult at crossroads, tree shrines, and boundary points — a cult with no Brahmanical sanction, no Sanskrit texts, no priestly intermediary. He argues these cults represent the pre-Aryan stratum of Indian religion: mother-goddess worship associated with boundaries, liminality, and agrarian fertility, later partially absorbed into Brahmanical categories (as Durgā, Ambā, etc.) but retaining a popular substratum.

**The Mhatoba cult:** A detailed fieldwork study of a god venerated in Maharashtra who displays attributes of both Bhairava (a fierce Śaiva deity) and a local ancestor-spirit. The cult demonstrates how pan-Indian Brahmanical categories (Śiva, Bhairava) overlay and partially absorb pre-existing regional cults without eliminating them.

**Goa village economy:** The final essay traces how the traditional Goan village commune preserved economic structures antedating colonial and even Brahmanical influence — demonstrating the persistence of pre-historical social formations into the historical period.

### Kosambi, *Culture and Civilisation of Ancient India* (1965)

Kosambi's most ambitious work — a complete materialist history of Indian civilization from the Palaeolithic to the early medieval period.

**Materialist historiography:** Kosambi's method is explicit: "the basis of history is the mode of production of the essential means of life." The forms of religion, law, art, and thought are determined by (or at least co-produced with) the way a society organizes labor, extracts surplus, and distributes resources. This does not mean culture is mere superstructure — Kosambi is sophisticated enough to recognize feedback — but it does mean that cultural change requires economic explanation.

**Food-gathering to food-production:** The transition from food-gathering (the Palaeolithic and Mesolithic hunter-gatherer societies) to food-production (Neolithic and Chalcolithic agriculture) is the foundational transformation of human civilization. For India, Kosambi identifies multiple regional trajectories of this transition, each leaving traces in later cultural forms.

**The Indus Valley as first urban formation:** Kosambi reads the Indus Valley Civilization (Harappa, Mohenjo-daro, ~2600–1900 BCE) as the first urban formation in the subcontinent — with a trading economy, a standardized writing system, and a social organization that appears to lack the palace-temple complex characteristic of Mesopotamian urbanism. He is skeptical of purely Aryan origin theories and argues for significant continuity between Indus Valley populations and later Indian cultures.

**Brahmanism as surplus legitimation:** The brahmin-kṣatriya alliance that organized classical Indian society is, for Kosambi, the institutional apparatus through which surplus is extracted from the agrarian base and legitimized as cosmic order. The varna system is not primarily a spiritual arrangement; it is a social-economic one, dressed in the language of dharma and cosmic hierarchy. This does not make the spiritual dimensions unreal, but it does explain why they take the form they do.

**Survivals methodology:** Kosambi's most original methodological contribution — tracking pre-historical social formations through "survivals" in folk practice, language, myth, and material culture. The Goddess-of-the-Crossroads cult, the pre-Aryan tree and stone worship, the persistence of matrilineal kinship in specific tribal groups — all are read as windows into social formations that predate the brahmanical literary record.
