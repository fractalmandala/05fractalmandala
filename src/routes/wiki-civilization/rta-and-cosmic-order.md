---
title: rta and cosmic order
id: 15
---

The Vedic concept of ṛta (from the root ṛ, "to move in a settled course") is the foundational principle of cosmic, natural, and moral order in the Ṛgveda — the law that makes the sun rise, rivers flow, and sacrifices bear fruit. It is not a deity but a dimension of reality itself, upheld by Varuṇa and enacted through the sacrifice. Paired with satya (truth), ṛta governs all three spheres of Vedic existence: the transcendent, the natural, and the human-ethical. The human access to ṛta is dhī — visionary insight, the illuminated cognition that the ṛṣis cultivated through tapas (contemplative ardor) — not ordinary thought, but a mode of seeing that participates in the cosmic order it perceives.

## Sources

### Miller, *Vision of Ṛta in the Vedas*

Miller's study reconstructs ṛta as a live metaphysical concept rather than a philological curiosity. The root ṛ ("to move in settled course") generates the idea of cosmic regularity — ṛta is what the universe does when it is functioning as it should. It operates across three spheres simultaneously: the transcendent (the divine councils, the seat of the gods), the natural (solar and atmospheric rhythms), and the human-ethical (truthful speech, right sacrifice, moral conduct). Satya (truth) is its correspondence at the human level — what ṛta is cosmically, satya is personally.

The companion concept is **dhī**: visionary insight, the specific cognitive mode of the Vedic seer. Miller is careful to distinguish dhī from ordinary mental activity (manas) or discursive thought. Dhī is the illuminated sight that grasps ṛta directly. It is not achieved by analysis but cultivated through **tapas** — contemplative exertion, the "heat" of sustained spiritual effort. The famous verse Miller centers his reading on: "From the seat of ṛta glows forth the visionary insight" — ṛta is not the object of dhī but its source and seat. The seer sees because he has been placed in alignment with the cosmic order, not because he has figured it out.

Tapas as a concept carries enormous weight: it is the primordial creative heat present before creation (as in the Nāsadīya Sūkta's "He practiced tapas, He created the world"), and also the specific discipline by which seers, ascetics, and sacrificers position themselves to access divine power. The Vedic tradition thus treats contemplative effort as cosmologically significant — the human meditator participates in the same generative heat that made the world.

### Panikkar, *The Vedic Experience (Mantramañjarī)*

Panikkar's anthology is not a commentary on the Vedas but what he calls a "living anthology" — a representation of the entire śruti (Ṛgveda, Atharvaveda, Yajurveda, Brāhmaṇas, Āraṇyakas, Upaniṣads, Gṛhya Sūtras) arranged not chronologically but according to the pattern of life itself: birth, growth, blossoming, fall, death, renewal. Subtitled *Mantramañjarī* ("cluster of blossoms of sacred words"), it was compiled over a decade from Varanasi by Panikkar in collaboration with Sanskrit scholars.

Panikkar's foundational claim: the Vedas are *apauruṣeya* — without human author — not as an embarrassment but as a philosophical insight. No one is the author of living words except the one who utters them now. The relationship between word and meaning is not artificial; it is intrinsic. You become the author of the Vedas when, through assimilation, you are able to utter them and they disclose their meaning for you. The śruti is that which is "heard" — and what is heard is not sound alone but all that there is to be heard, perceived, realized. Our own process of discovery is part of the revelation itself.

The central characterization of Vedic humanity: **Vedic Man is fundamentally a celebrating Man** — not a philosopher, moralist, or devotee, but a *celebrant*. He concelebrates with the whole universe, taking his place in the cosmic sacrifice in which all the gods are engaged. The Vedic experience does not carry a doctrinal message; it carries "a universal form of human celebration."

On the Nāsadīya Sūkta (RV X.129) — the hymn of origins — Panikkar identifies three structural moments: **Solitude** (the One awakens alone, discovers loneliness, and from that arises desire for another), **Sacrifice** (Prajāpati dismembers himself in an act of self-immolation from which the world emerges — creation as sacrifice, with no external recipient), and **Integration** (the creature must reconstruct Prajāpati's body through participation in the sacrifice — Man's role is not worship in exchange for release, but co-creation). From ṛta and satya and kāma (desire) and tapas (ardor) — both not motives for action but indwelling principles of reality — the cosmos emerges and sustains itself.
