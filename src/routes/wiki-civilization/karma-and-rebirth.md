---
title: karma and rebirth
id: 11
---

Karma began as a technical term for the Vedic ritual act and its inherent efficacy — the sacrifice produces results because that is what sacrifice does, not because a deity intervenes. Over a millennium, this ritual-causal meaning expanded into a moral-causal cosmology in which every intentional act leaves a residue (saṃskāra) that shapes future experience across multiple lifetimes. By the time of the Mahābhārata, karma had become the operative mechanism of a universe organized around moral accountability, and it continued to evolve through Dharmaśāstra codification, Advaita dissolution, and the Buddhist reinterpretation that retained karma while rejecting the self that carries it.

## Sources

### Doniger (ed.), *Karma and Rebirth in Indian Traditions*

This edited volume collects essays on karma across Vedic, Epic, Dharmaśāstra, Buddhist, Jain, and folk contexts — tracking its semantic evolution rather than treating it as a single stable concept.

**Vedic origins:** Early Vedic texts use *karman* to mean the ritual act in its technical sense. The sacrifice works automatically — the connection between act and result is built into the cosmic structure. There is no moral dimension yet; it is causal mechanics, not ethics.

**Epic transformation:** The Mahābhārata is the crucible in which karma becomes a moral-causal system, and it does so while preserving deep moral ambiguity. Karma explains why good people suffer and why the wicked prosper — but the Mahābhārata is equally interested in the exceptions, the cases where karmic logic fails or bends. The divine suspension of karma (grace, divine favor) is a persistent theme alongside its affirmation.

**Dharmaśāstra codification:** The legal literature (Manusmṛti, etc.) systematizes karma as the engine of varna and jāti — birth into a particular social station reflects accumulated past-life merit. This is the most politically charged deployment of karma: it simultaneously explains social hierarchy and appears to justify it.

**Niṣkāma karma:** The Bhagavad Gīta's intervention: action performed without attachment to its fruits (niṣkāma karma) does not generate karmic residue. The problem of karma is not action itself but the ego's investment in results. This move simultaneously preserves the concept of dharmic action and offers a path out of the karmic cycle for the actor within the world.

**Dividual persons (Marriott):** The volume includes McKim Marriott's anthropological argument that the Indian self is not an individual (an undivided monad) but a "dividual" — a porous entity that transacts substance with others, absorbs and emits coded matter through food, touch, ritual. Karma, in this frame, is not a private moral ledger but a field of substance-exchange that crosses bodily boundaries.

### McClelland, *Encyclopedia of Reincarnation and Karma*

McClelland's encyclopedia provides the global comparative framework within which Indian karma sits — along with a careful disambiguation of overlapping terms.

**Rebirth vs. reincarnation:** McClelland distinguishes these carefully. *Rebirth* refers broadly to any continuity of personhood or life-force across deaths (compatible with Buddhist anatman). *Reincarnation* implies a substantial soul that transmigrates intact between bodies. Indian traditions use both models, and the distinction matters theologically: the Buddhist anātman doctrine makes karma work without a persistent soul — causal continuity without substantial identity.

**Buddhist anatman problem:** How can karma operate if there is no continuous self? The Buddhist answer is that the "stream" of consciousness carries causal imprints (vāsanā, saṃskāra) forward without requiring a soul as carrier — like a flame passed from candle to candle, different yet causally continuous.

**Global distribution:** The encyclopedia maps rebirth beliefs across cultures — West African, Greek (Pythagorean, Platonic), Celtic, indigenous American, and Spiritist traditions — arguing that the concept arises in enough separate cultural contexts that it cannot be reduced to diffusion from India. Some form of rebirth belief appears to be one of the most widespread religious intuitions in human history.

**Karma's ethical tensions:** McClelland identifies the core ethical ambiguity: karma appears to explain suffering while simultaneously legitimizing it. If someone suffers, it is because of past-life actions — a logic that can function as victim-blaming and as social-hierarchy justification. This tension has generated ongoing debate within and outside Indian traditions. Reform movements (Buddhism, bhakti, social reform Hinduism) have frequently attacked karma's social deployment even while retaining the metaphysical concept.

**Modern Western resurgence:** New Age adoption of karma has decoupled it from rebirth and reincarnation and redefined it as immediate moral reciprocity ("what goes around comes around") — a significant semantic transformation that retains the word while abandoning the doctrine.

### *The Architecture of Transmigration: Archaeological and Textual Origins of Karma and Punarjanma* (res-karma.md)

This research document reconstructs the emergence of karma and rebirth doctrine from prehistoric funerary evidence through the Upanishadic synthesis, arguing that by 500 BCE a "fully developed tripartite doctrine" of karma, rebirth, and liberation was in place — the product of Vedic ritualism's internal pressure plus the shock of the Śramaṇa movement.

**Prehistoric funerary foundations:** The earliest evidence comes from burial practices that presuppose a distinction between the physical body and a persisting essence. Mehrgarh (Balochistan, from the 7th millennium BCE) and Ganga Valley sites (Mahadaha, Sarai Nahar Rai) show intentional burials with grave goods — tools, ornaments, food vessels — positioned to ensure the deceased's wellbeing in a post-mortem journey. Body orientations (E-W, NW-SE) suggest a structured cosmological mapping of the death transition. Chalcolithic urn burials at sites like Farmana read the ceramic jar as a "womb" — a protective cocoon for the soul preparing for its next transition, which is a striking material parallel to later Upanishadic descriptions of the soul's re-entry into the father and mother to be reborn.

**Rakhigarhi necropolis — divergent eschatologies:** The Rakhigarhi burial ground (RGR-7) reveals three concurrent burial types: primary extended inhumations (89%), secondary partial-skeletal burials, and symbolic cenotaphs — prepared pits with pottery and ornaments but no human remains. The cenotaphs indicate that the ritual performance mattered more than the physical body's presence, pointing toward a growing dualism between the material self and an immaterial essence. Differential grave goods (gold and exotic beads predominantly in female graves; simpler goods in children's graves) suggest the afterlife destiny was assessed through accumulated social status — an early structural ancestor of karmic merit.

**Sinauli — the warrior afterlife (~1900–1800 BCE):** Royal coffin burials with copper-decorated chariots, antenna swords, shields, and ritual utensils show a militarized elite conceptualizing the afterlife as a continuation of earthly warrior status. The dead were *equipped* for an active post-mortem role. This warrior theology provides the sociological substrate for the "secret of the Kṣatriyas" that the early Upanishads describe — rebirth doctrine as a teaching for warriors who need metaphysical grounds for fearlessness in battle. See [[prehistoric-india-and-archaeology]].

**Ṛgvedic eschatology — no rebirth, but re-death anxiety:** In the Ṛgveda, the dominant goal is *amṛta* (immortality after a single fruitful life), not cyclical rebirth. Agni conveys the dead to the Fathers; Yama rules a happy celestial realm where one receives a "second body." There is no systematic doctrine of repeated earthly birth. Scholars like Koenraad Elst argue that modern attempts to read *punarjanma* into the Ṛgveda project Purāṇic categories onto texts whose actual vision is incompatible with reincarnation.

**Brāhmaṇa transition — punarmṛtyu (re-death):** The Brāhmaṇas (~900–700 BCE) introduce the critical conceptual turn: heaven is not permanent. Merit from sacrifice has a limited "fuel supply"; once it is exhausted, the soul faces *punarmṛtyu* — a second death in the celestial realm — and must return to earth. The *Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa* states explicitly: "in being born, man is born as a debt to death; in that he sacrifices, he redeems himself from death." This transforms heaven into a temporary way-station and generates the need for a means of permanent escape — the conceptual slot that will be filled by knowledge (*jñāna*) rather than sacrifice.

**Upanishadic revolution — the ethicization of karma (~800–500 BCE):** The *Bṛhadāraṇyaka* and *Chāndogya Upaniṣads* relocate punarmṛtyu from the celestial to the material world and reframe the mechanism: it is no longer ritual merit that determines one's fate but *intentional action* (karma in the moral sense). The "Five-Fire Doctrine" (*Pañcāgni Vidyā*) — the soul's journey through rain, earth, plant, animal, and back to human — is the first systematic description of rebirth as an ethically determined cycle. *Yājñavalkya's* teaching to Janaka presents *ātman* as the knowing subject that persists through death, dreaming, and deep sleep — and that transmigrates according to its accumulated intentions. Liberation (*mokṣa*) is the cessation of the transmigration cycle through direct knowledge of ātman's identity with Brahman.
