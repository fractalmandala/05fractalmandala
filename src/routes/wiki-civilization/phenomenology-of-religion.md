The phenomenology of religion attempts to describe what religious experience is like from the inside — what structure it has, what its essential features are — without reducing it to something else (psychology, sociology, economics). Its foundational claim, associated most strongly with van der Leeuw but also with Otto, Eliade, and others, is that religion has a distinctive object: the "holy" or "sacred" or "power" that confronts the religious subject as something utterly different from ordinary experience, something that exceeds explanation in merely natural terms. The methodological debate that runs through the field concerns whether this claim is defensible: can the phenomenologist bracket his or her own commitments and attend purely to the structure of another's experience, or is the category of "religion" itself a scholarly construction that distorts what it describes?

## Sources

### van der Leeuw, *Phenomenology of Religion*

Van der Leeuw's *Phänomenologie der Religion* (1933, revised 1963) is the founding text of the phenomenological approach to the study of religion. Its central claim: **power (Macht)** is the fundamental datum of religious experience — prior to all theological articulation, ritual elaboration, or moral teaching. Before gods, before doctrine, before prayer — there is the encounter with an overwhelming, mysterious, and compelling force that exceeds ordinary human measure.

**Power (Macht):** The religious person does not first develop a theology and then experience its object; they first encounter something that demands response — a presence, a force, a "wholly other" — and theology comes later. Van der Leeuw surveys the range of objects that have served as foci of this power: stones, trees, rivers, animals, ancestors, gods. What distinguishes the religious object is not its ontological category but its functional quality — it is charged with Macht.

**Sacred and profane:** The sacred is experienced as categorically different from the profane — not merely more powerful, but differently structured, subject to different rules of approach and handling. The taboo (the object that must not be touched, approached, or named) is the clearest expression of this boundary: it marks off the sacred from the ordinary not because the sacred is dangerous in a merely physical sense, but because the distinction of kinds must be maintained.

**Taboo, magic, prayer:** Van der Leeuw maps three modes of relating to sacred power: taboo (avoiding the sacred), magic (coercing or manipulating it), and prayer (entering into relation with it as a "Thou"). These are not chronological stages but permanent possibilities — each reflecting a different existential orientation toward the overwhelming.

**The wholly other:** Following Rudolf Otto's *Das Heilige*, van der Leeuw describes the sacred as *mysterium tremendum et fascinans* — a mystery that simultaneously terrifies (tremendum) and attracts (fascinans). Neither response alone captures it; the holy is both repellent and magnetic, both dangerous and desirable.

**Sui generis religion:** The methodological consequence: religion cannot be explained away by psychology (it is not merely projection), sociology (it is not merely social cohesion), or economics (it is not merely a tool of control). It has its own distinctive character and must be approached on its own terms. This is the "sui generis" position — religion is irreducible.

### Tylor, *Primitive Culture*

E.B. Tylor's *Primitive Culture* (1871) is the founding text of the anthropological study of religion — and the foil against which almost all subsequent phenomenology of religion has defined itself.

**Animism as minimum definition:** Tylor defines religion by its lowest common denominator: the "belief in spiritual beings." This is animism — the attribution of souls, spirits, or personhood to natural phenomena. All religions, from the most "primitive" to the most sophisticated, share this element. This minimum definition allows cross-cultural comparison but defines religion so broadly that it includes virtually everything supernatural.

**The doctrine of survivals:** Tylor's most methodologically influential contribution — the idea that practices, customs, and beliefs survive in later cultural formations as fossils of earlier social stages. The European peasant who leaves out food for the spirits of the dead is performing a "survival" of Palaeolithic ancestor worship. This methodology (used, with modifications, by Kosambi in [[indian-history-and-civilization]]) allows the anthropologist to read backward from contemporary practice to prehistoric belief.

**Evolutionary anthropology:** Tylor arranges cultures on an evolutionary timeline from "primitive" (animistic) to "civilized" (monotheistic, then rational-scientific). This framework is deeply problematic — it positions contemporary "primitive" peoples as representatives of humanity's past — but it generated the comparative method that subsequent scholars, even critics, have had to grapple with.
