The Goddess (*Devī*) tradition in India presents a historical puzzle: despite abundant archaeological evidence for goddess worship from the Neolithic onward, a fully articulated literary theology of a supreme, autonomous female divine does not appear until the early centuries of the Common Era. The *Devi Mahatmya* — chapters 81–93 of the *Mārkaṇḍeya Purāṇa*, composed roughly between 400–600 CE — is the text in which this theology crystallizes. Thomas Coburn's study of it argues that the text does not *invent* the Goddess but *defines* her: gathering centuries of Vedic imagery, epic mythology, local cults, and philosophical concepts into a single, unified vision in which the Goddess is not one deity among many but the power (*śakti*) that animates all deities and the ultimate ground of all existence.

---

## Sources

### Coburn, T.B., *Devi-Mahatmya: The Crystallization of the Goddess Tradition* (1984)

**The crystallization thesis.** The *Devi Mahatmya* is not a point of origin but a point of convergence. Earlier traditions — Mother Goddess figurines at Mohenjo-daro and Rakhigarhi (see [[prehistoric-india-and-archaeology]]), fragmentary Vedic feminine figures like Uṣas and Vāc, epic portrayals of Durgā and Kālī, and hundreds of local cult goddesses — exist before the text. What the text accomplishes is their reorganization into a coherent theological grammar: the Goddess as creator, sustainer, destroyer, and liberator, all at once.

**Sanskritization as mutual transformation.** Coburn resists the standard "non-Aryan substrate absorbed into Brahmanism" model. The *Devi Mahatmya* is not a story of absorption from below; it is a story of mutual reconfiguration. Local and non-Sanskritic elements are given Sanskritic form, but the Sanskritic tradition is simultaneously transformed — the entire theological hierarchy is reorganized around a female center. This is Gonda's "conservative transformation" (see [[cultural-memory-and-tradition]]) operating at its most dramatic scale.

**The structure of the text.** Coburn reads the *Devi Mahatmya* through three interlocking registers: epithets, myths, and hymns. Epithets accumulate the Goddess's identity through names; myths enact that identity through narrative; hymns articulate it philosophically. These three levels are not separate but mutually reinforcing — the same theological claim appears in all three modes simultaneously.

### The Epithets: A Theology Assembled from Names

The *Devi Mahatmya*'s strategy of naming is not decorative; it is constructive. Each epithet is a compressed history — a localized cult figure, a philosophical category, or a divine function that the text absorbs, universalizes, and reorients around the Goddess.

**Candikā** — fierce, overwhelming, destructive energy. The name that opens the text and governs its tone: the Goddess is the one who acts decisively when the cosmic order is threatened. Her violence is purposeful, not chaotic.

**Ambikā** — the universal mother. Maternal imagery is retained but universalized: she is not the mother of a particular clan or deity, but of all beings. Ferocity and maternal care coexist in the same figure.

**Kālī** — the Goddess as time and dissolution. Unlike Candikā, whose destruction is functional (responding to a specific threat), Kālī is ontological: she *is* the principle of dissolution embedded in reality. She emerges within the text from the body of another form of the Goddess, as if time and death are latent within every manifestation of power.

**Mahāmāyā** — the Goddess as cosmic illusion and cosmic revelation simultaneously. She is the power that veils and the power that unveils. She governs both bondage and liberation. This epithet is where the text's theology most directly engages the philosophical tradition: Māyā, drawn from both Vedic and later Vedāntic usage, is here feminized and personalized.

**Durgā** — the fortress and the passage through danger. She presides not merely over protection but over the structure of difficulty itself — the trials and crises through which cosmic and individual existence must pass.

**Nārāyaṇī, Vaiṣṇavī, Māheśvarī, Brāhmaṇī** — a systematic series of epithets in which the powers of the major male deities (Viṣṇu, Śiva, Brahmā) are reinterpreted as expressions of the Goddess's own Śakti. The Goddess does not compete with these deities; she subsumes them. Their power is real — but it is real because it is hers.

**Śakti** — the pivot of the entire epithet system. The Goddess is not merely a bearer of power; she *is* power itself, the fundamental energy that animates all existence. This identification allows every divine function in the pantheon to be re-read as a facet of a single underlying force.

**Prakṛti** — the Goddess identified with the fundamental material principle of the Sāṃkhya philosophical system. She is the ground of material existence. The identification simultaneously absorbs a major philosophical category and deconstructs the Sāṃkhya dualism by making Prakṛti conscious and personal.

**Mahādevī** — the culminating epithet: "great goddess." All the preceding names, with their diverse histories, converge into this one. The totalization is explicit.

One further pair is striking: the text includes both **Lakṣmī** (prosperity, fortune) and **Alakṣmī** (misfortune, inauspiciousness) within the Goddess's identity. She is the source of both. Nothing is outside her scope — not even what is unwelcome.

### The Three Myths: The Theology Enacted

**1. Madhu and Kaitabha** — the most subtle episode. Viṣṇu lies in cosmic sleep (*Yoganidrā*). The sleep is the Goddess herself, enveloping him. The demons arise; Viṣṇu cannot act until the Goddess releases him. The theological point is established before any battle occurs: the highest male deity is inactive without her. She is not his power — she is the condition of his power.

**2. Mahīṣāsura** — the dramatic center of the text. The demon Mahīṣa (the buffalo-demon) has displaced all the gods from their positions. In response, the gods emit their *tejas* (divine energies), which converge into a single radiant form — the Goddess. She is the sum of all divine powers, and simultaneously something more than their sum: a transcendent synthesis. The gods then *give her their weapons*, acknowledging that what they held was always hers. The battle is prolonged and shape-shifting (Mahīṣa transforms repeatedly — buffalo, lion, elephant, human), and the Goddess matches every transformation. Her slaying of Mahīṣa is the most iconic image in the Śākta visual tradition.

**3. Śumbha and Niśumbha** — the most philosophically complex episode. The demons demand the Goddess as their consort — they want to *possess* her power. She refuses. As the battle unfolds, the Goddess manifests multiple forms (the Mātṛkās — the "mothers"), each corresponding to a divine power. Then, after their purpose is served, she reabsorbs them all. When Śumbha challenges her for using allies, she replies: *there are no others — all those forms were me*. This is the theological climax. The multiplicity of the divine is not denied; it is shown to originate from and return to a single source. Unity does not suppress plurality; it grounds it.

### The Three Hymns: The Theology Articulated

Each myth is followed by a hymn that translates its narrative into explicit theological statement.

The **first hymn** (after Madhu-Kaitabha) foregrounds Mahāmāyā: the Goddess as the power of cosmic sleep, illusion, and awakening. She is present in memory, hunger, consciousness, sleep — the fundamental operations of both cosmos and individual mind.

The **Śakra hymn** (after Mahīṣa, led by Indra) is the most elaborate and the one most explicitly concerned with dharma. The Goddess is praised as the source of all divine powers; as intelligence (*buddhi*), prosperity (*śrī*), faith (*śraddhā*), and modesty (*lajjā*); and as the one who sustains cosmic order and destroys those who threaten it.

The **final hymn** (after Śumbha-Niśumbha) brings the articulation to its highest abstraction. The Goddess is the ultimate reality behind all forms — *Muktihetu*, the cause of liberation. She is present in all beings. She is self-sufficient: the gods depend on her; she depends on nothing. The framework has been redefined around her.

---

### Key Theses

The *Devi Mahatmya* represents crystallization, not origin — it defines the Goddess by bringing divergent strands into a unified theology. That theology rests on the primacy of Śakti: the Goddess is not the highest deity but the power in which all deities participate. The text's structural logic is one of unity underlying multiplicity — she manifests as many and returns to one, and this process is not a problem but the very structure of reality. And the text achieves totalization: nothing — cosmic, ritual, psychological, ethical — falls outside her scope.

## Connections Across the Wiki

**Prehistoric continuities:** The Mother Goddess figurines at Mohenjo-daro and Harappa, and the Baghor I shrine (~9,000 BCE), establish that goddess veneration is among the oldest religious practices in the subcontinent. The *Devi Mahatmya* is the literary culmination of a tradition whose material roots are Neolithic. See [[prehistoric-india-and-archaeology]].

**Śiva's absorptions parallel the Goddess's:** The same mechanism — a non-Vedic deity overwhelming the sacrificial order and forcing theological accommodation — operates in both the Dakṣa-sacrifice episode (Śiva's triumph) and the *Devi Mahatmya* (the Goddess's supremacy). Bhattacharji's account of Śiva's seven absorptions and Coburn's account of the Goddess's epithet-integrations are structurally identical processes. See [[shiva]].

**Conservative transformation:** Gonda's principle — that Indian religious forms evolve through cumulative accretion and recontextualization rather than replacement — is the underlying logic of the *Devi Mahatmya*. Vedic elements (Svāhā, Svadhā, Uṣas) are retained but reorganized around the Goddess. See [[cultural-memory-and-tradition]].

**Polycentric polytheism:** The *Devi Mahatmya* is a paradigm case of what Daniélou and Butler describe as the Hindu tradition's capacity to treat any deity as the supreme one within a given devotional context. The text does not argue against Viṣṇu or Śiva; it recenters reality around the Goddess while allowing them to remain real. See [[polytheism-and-divine-plurality]] and [[hinduism-sectarian-traditions]].

**Mahāmāyā and Upanishadic Brahman:** The Goddess as Mahāmāyā — the power that simultaneously veils and reveals ultimate reality — stands in a complex relationship to the Upanishadic *Brahman* and the Advaita concept of *māyā*. Śaṅkara's *māyā* is ultimately illusory; the Goddess's *māyā* is real, dynamic, and personal. The *Devi Mahatmya* represents one response to the Upanishadic metaphysics that refuses the impersonal solution. See [[yoga-and-liberation]].
