Śiva is the most paradoxical figure in the Hindu pantheon: simultaneously the destroyer and the ascetic, the erotic and the celibate, the outsider and the supreme deity. His roots lie in the Ṛgvedic Rudra — a feared, propitiated figure who stands apart from the sacrificial order — overlaid with pre-Aryan cult elements traceable to the Indus Valley, and subsequently absorbed virtually every divine function in the Indian pantheon through a process of theological expansion that culminated in his identification as the Absolute. Two scholarly works give complementary accounts of this figure: Kramrisch's phenomenological reconstruction of Śiva's theological depths, and Bhattacharji's historical analysis of how Rudra became Śiva.

## Sources

### Kramrisch, *The Presence of Shiva*

Kramrisch opens with the primordial scene: the Father-Daughter union — Prajāpati (the Creator) pursuing his daughter — and the Archer who shoots an arrow through the act. The Archer is Rudra. This "uncreated" act in the interstice of no-time is the moment Śiva enters mythic consciousness: he is the one who interrupts the incestuous surplus of creation, who enforces the boundary between what is and what must not be. He is not present at creation; he breaks into it.

**Rudra as Agni's fierce form:** In the Vedic sacrificial system, Rudra is identified with the most dangerous and unpredictable aspect of Agni — the fire that escapes the altar. The Śatarudriya (hymn to the hundred Rudras in the Yajurveda) is a propitiatory text, naming Rudra in all his aspects across all domains of the natural world — in the forest, the crossroads, the battlefield, the cremation ground — to ward off his harm. This is not worship in the devotional sense; it is negotiation with an overwhelming presence.

**Linga theology:** Kramrisch's most sustained analysis concerns the Śivalinga — the column of light (jyotirlinga) that appears to Brahma and Viṣṇu as a flame without beginning or end. She distinguishes two modes of the linga: *niṣkala* (without parts — the absolute, undifferentiated Śiva, formless light) and *sakala* (with parts — the iconic, anthropomorphic Śiva with attributes). The linga is not a phallus in the first instance; it is an axis mundi, an upright column that connects the realms. The sexual dimension (the linga embedded in the yoni) belongs to the sakala mode and represents the union of transcendence and immanence.

**The castration myth:** A group of sages, disturbed in their forest meditation, curse Rudra; his phallus falls to the earth and burns everything it touches until properly installed and worshipped. Kramrisch reads this not as a humiliation but as a paradox of power: the ascetic's total renunciation of sexuality becomes the source of infinite creative energy. The fallen linga, worshipped by all, becomes the most potent icon in the tradition.

**Ardhanārīśvara:** The half-male, half-female form of Śiva-Pārvatī represents the coincidentia oppositorum — the resolution of all binary opposition into a single form. Śiva is masculine consciousness, Śakti is feminine creative power; their union is not a joining of two separate things but the recognition that the distinction was always secondary.

**Astronomical interpretation:** Kramrisch pursues an astronomical reading of the Rudra mythology, identifying Sirius (the hunter, Mṛgavyādha) and Orion (the Father/Prajāpati as the constellation) in the primordial scene — a cosmological encoding that places the Śiva mythology at the intersection of sky-observation and metaphysical speculation.

### Bhattacharji, *Indian Theogony* — Rudra-Śiva sections

Bhattacharji tracks Rudra's trajectory from Ṛgvedic outsider to supreme deity through the lens of historical absorption. Rudra appears in the Ṛgveda as a non-Aryan interloper — feared, propitiated with anxiety ("don't harm us, spare our cattle"), never quite integrated into the sacrificial fellowship. His epithets are simultaneously terrifying and healing; *Śiva* (auspicious) is an apotropaic title, not a description of his nature. His physical description — tawny, matted-haired, wearing animal skins, carrying a bow, haunting mountains and cremation grounds — signals a deity whose template lies outside the Vedic ṛṣi tradition.

The non-Aryan problem is sharpest in the linga cult: the Ṛgveda itself contains hostile references to *śiśnadevāḥ* — "those whose god is the phallus" — as an insult aimed at non-Aryan tribes. The complete reversal of this hostility, such that the Śivaliṅga became the central icon of one of the world's largest religious traditions, is the single most dramatic instance of non-Aryan religious material overcoming Vedic resistance.

Bhattacharji traces Śiva's ascent through seven specific absorptions: Skanda (the war-god, whose parentage is transferred to Śiva), Gaṇapati (made his son), Kubera (made his companion), Agni (crematory fire fully absorbed), Puṣan (cattle-deity, his followers absorbed), Indra (heroic functions absorbed), and the Mother Goddess complex (Umā/Pārvatī/Durgā/Kālī). Each absorption is documented through specific textual moments. The Dakṣa-sacrifice episode mythologizes this historical process: Śiva is excluded from the Vedic order; its destruction and his subsequent primacy represents the moment when the non-Aryan cult overwhelmed Vedic sacrificial orthodoxy and forced the tradition to accommodate him on his own terms.

The Paśupati seal from the Indus Valley — a seated yogi figure surrounded by animals — is treated cautiously but referenced as circumstantial evidence for a pre-Aryan prototype that eventually became Paśupati (Lord of Animals), one of Śiva's oldest and most stable epithets.
