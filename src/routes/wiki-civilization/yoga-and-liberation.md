Yoga is not a single system but a family of practices — physical, contemplative, devotional, intellectual — united by the root *yuj* ("to yoke, to unite, to harness") and by the goal of transforming or transcending the ordinary condition of the person. Its history extends from shamanic antecedents in the pre-Vedic period through Patañjali's classical formulation to the Advaita Vedānta position that the self requiring liberation never existed in the way it thought it did. Liberation (mokṣa, mukti, kaivalya) is variously understood as the isolation of pure consciousness from matter, the dissolution of individual selfhood into Brahman, or the recognition that the world-as-experienced is a projection of mind that can be seen through.

## Sources

### Feuerstein, *The Yoga Tradition*

Feuerstein's comprehensive historical survey treats yoga as one of the most sustained and methodologically rigorous investigations into the nature of consciousness ever conducted.

**Etymology and scope:** The root *yuj* has two meanings in Sanskrit: "to yoke" (harness, discipline, unite) and "to enter into samādhi" (absorption, union with the object of contemplation). Different yoga traditions emphasize one or the other. The former underlies the classical Yoga Sūtras model (yoga as the disciplining of the mind); the latter underlies the Advaita and Tāntric models (yoga as absorption, dissolution of subject-object distinction).

**Patañjali's citta-vṛtti-nirodha:** The Yoga Sūtras' definition — "yoga is the cessation of the fluctuations of the mind-stuff (citta)" — is the most precise formulation of what Patañjali means by yoga. The ordinary mind is characterized by continuous *vṛttis* (fluctuations, modifications) — perceptions, inferences, memories, imaginings, sleep-states. Each vṛtti leaves a *saṃskāra* (impression, residue) that generates more vṛttis. The yogin works to progressively still this process until the pure witness-consciousness (puruṣa) is revealed as what it always was, uncontaminated by the vṛttis it was mistaken for.

**The schools:** Feuerstein surveys Jñāna Yoga (knowledge/inquiry), Bhakti Yoga (devotion), Karma Yoga (action without attachment), Rāja Yoga (Patañjali's eight-limbed system), Haṭha Yoga (bodily disciplines), Mantra Yoga (sacred sound), Tantra (transgressive and inclusive), Laya Yoga (absorption), and the shamanic proto-yoga that precedes all of them. Each school has a different diagnosis of the human problem and a different cure, but all share the recognition that the ordinary state of human consciousness is not its optimal or final state.

**Shamanic origins:** Feuerstein argues that the impulse toward ecstasis — leaving ordinary consciousness behind — is detectable in Ṛgvedic figures (the keśin, the long-haired ascetic of RV 10.136), in the Vedic concept of the wandering, wind-riding muni, and in figures from the Harappan period. The "seated yogi" seal from Mohenjo-daro is frequently cited. The point is not genetic derivation but the antiquity of the contemplative impulse in the subcontinent.

**Guru-disciple transmission:** All yoga traditions treat the guru-disciple relationship as essential — the transmission of yoga is not merely informational but experiential, involving a direct transfer of śakti (power) and state from teacher to student. This is why yoga texts consistently emphasize finding a qualified teacher over learning from books.

### *Laghu Yoga Vasishtha*

The *Laghu Yoga Vasishtha* (the abridged version of the vast *Yoga Vasishtha*) is one of the most sustained presentations of Advaita Vedānta philosophy through narrative — the teachings of the sage Vasiṣṭha to the young prince Rāma on the nature of reality, consciousness, and liberation.

**Manodṛśyam idaṃ sarvam:** The central thesis — "the whole world is an object of the mind" — is stated and defended through hundreds of illustrative stories. The cosmos is not an independent external reality that consciousness passively perceives; it is a projection of mind. This is the *dṛṣṭi-sṛṣṭi-vāda* ("the doctrine that seeing creates creation") — the observed universe comes into existence through the act of observation, and the observer (jīva) is itself a construction of the same mind-stuff.

**Vāsanās (latent tendencies):** The mechanism that sustains the illusion of an independent world is *vāsanā* — the accumulated residue of past experiences and intentions that pre-shapes perception. The cycle of saṃsāra runs on vāsanās: past experiences generate mental impressions that generate future experiences that generate more impressions. Liberation is the thinning and eventual elimination of vāsanā, at which point the projected world is no longer experienced as independent.

**Vairāgya (dispassion) as catalyst:** The text emphasizes vairāgya — not aversion or hatred of the world, but a clear-eyed recognition that the world as experienced does not deliver what it appears to promise. This recognition is not itself liberation; it is the condition that makes inquiry possible. Without some degree of dispassion, the mind remains too invested in its projections to step back and examine them.

**Liberation through recognition:** The *Laghu Yoga Vasishtha* does not describe liberation as an achievement but as a recognition — the discovery that one is already what one was seeking. The self (Ātman) is not something to be attained but something to be uncovered beneath the layers of misidentification with body, mind, and ego. The practice of inquiry (vicāra) — asking "who am I?" or "what is the nature of this 'I' that experiences?" — is the primary method.
