Modern Western scholarship on Indian texts — particularly the German Indological tradition of the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries — was not a neutral enterprise. It emerged from Protestant theological debates, Romantic nationalism, and the racial ideologies of European modernity, and it imposed these frameworks on Sanskrit literature under the cover of "scientific" philology. The critique of this tradition, most comprehensively mounted by Vishwa Adluri and Joydeep Bagchee in *The Nay Science* (2014), argues that the historical-critical method falsified Indian texts by fragmenting them into hypothetical layers, projecting European theological categories onto them, and evacuating their philosophical significance in the name of historical reconstruction.

## Sources

### Adluri, V. & Bagchee, J., *The Nay Science: A History of German Indology* (2014)

*The Nay Science* is a sustained intellectual history and philosophical critique of German Indology — specifically the application of the historical-critical method to the *Mahābhārata* and the Bhagavad Gītā. The title inverts the discipline's self-description as a *Wissenschaft* (science): the authors argue it was, in fact, a "nay science" — a pseudo-scientific enterprise disguising theological and ideological commitments as scholarly objectivity.

**The Protestant genealogy of the method.** The historical-critical method did not originate in Sanskrit philology. It emerged from Protestant Biblical criticism, where scholars sought to reconcile faith with modern historical consciousness by treating scripture as a historically conditioned document rather than revelation. When German Indologists transferred this method to Indian texts, they carried its theological inheritance with them — the assumption that a "pure original" text existed beneath later priestly accretions, mirroring Protestant narratives of corruption by institutional religion. This genealogy was never acknowledged; the method was presented as universal and neutral.

**The search for the "Urepos."** Early German Indologists — Lassen, Weber, Holtzmann — approached the *Mahābhārata* as a corrupted text that needed to be stripped back to an original heroic epic (*Urepos*). The current form was treated as degenerate, contaminated by Brahmanical theological additions. This narrative mirrored Romantic nationalist preoccupations: the search for pure, original cultural forms, the valorization of heroic antiquity, and suspicion of priestly complexity. Different scholars produced incompatible reconstructions, exposing the speculative nature of the enterprise.

**The German identity problem.** Holtzmann's interpretation of the *Mahābhārata* was simultaneously a statement about German identity. The "original" epic he reconstructed was quasi-European in ethos — heroic, pre-theological, racially congruent with emerging Indo-European theory. Indian textual history became a proxy space in which European theological conflicts (Protestant vs. Catholic, original purity vs. institutional corruption) were re-enacted. The method produced results that served ideological purposes while appearing scientific.

**The Gītā fragmented.** The same logic applied to the Bhagavad Gītā. Rather than reading it as a unified philosophical text, German scholars treated it as a composite of incompatible doctrinal strata. Garbe sought an original theistic core; others found pantheism; still others argued the whole Gītā was a later epic interpolation. Each reconstruction reflected the scholar's own philosophical preferences dressed as historical discovery. The result was a proliferation of mutually incompatible "original Gītās," none grounded in verifiable evidence.

**Methodological autonomy and circularity.** Over time, the historical-critical method detached from the texts it purported to study and became self-referential. It generated its own problems, validated its own conclusions, and defined what counted as legitimate scholarship. Scholars became more concerned with demonstrating methodological correctness than with understanding the texts. The method was circular: it began from expectations about what "original" texts looked like and then produced reconstructions that confirmed those expectations.

**The positivism problem.** German Indology sought to align itself with the natural sciences — claiming empirical rigor, objectivity, and verifiability. But texts are not natural objects. Their meanings cannot be directly observed or measured. The method's key claims — the existence of earlier textual layers, the identification of interpolations, the reconstruction of original forms — are speculative inferences, not empirical findings. The rhetoric of science legitimized interpretations that were in fact contingent and ideologically shaped.

**Fragmentation as philosophical loss.** By breaking texts into hypothetical layers, the method destroyed their internal coherence as philosophical works. The *Mahābhārata* ceased to be a meditation on dharma, fate, and action and became an unsolved problem of source criticism. The Gītā's unity was treated as an illusion to be dissolved rather than a structure to be understood. The discipline produced technically elaborate scholarship that was philosophically superficial.

**Gandhi as counter-example.** The book's conclusion pivots to Gandhi's reading of the Gītā as an alternative model. Gandhi did not attempt to reconstruct an original text or separate authentic from interpolated material. He read the text as it exists, treating the battlefield as a symbol of the moral struggle within the self and the dialogue between Krishna and Arjuna as direct guidance for living. This approach — engaged, integrative, philosophically serious — is what the historical-critical method systematically disabled. The text becomes a *source of insight* rather than an object of analysis.

**The call for philosophical re-engagement.** The book's constructive implication is not that historical inquiry should be abandoned but that it must be subordinated to philosophical understanding. Indian texts like the *Mahābhārata* and the Gītā are interventions in fundamental questions about action, duty, liberation, and the nature of reality. To read them adequately requires engaging those questions directly, not treating the texts as puzzles of textual stratification.

## Connections Across the Wiki

The critique mounted by Adluri and Bagchee connects to several themes that run through this knowledge base:

**Phenomenology vs. historicism.** Van der Leeuw (see [[phenomenology-of-religion]]) distinguished between phenomenological engagement with religious power (*Macht*) and the reduction of religion to its historical conditions. The Nay Science's critique of German Indology is essentially a critique of historicism applied to texts — the reduction of meaning to origin. Eliade makes a parallel argument about the "terror of history" (see [[sacred-space-and-time]]): historical consciousness dissolves the sacred into mere event.

**Conservative transformation vs. degenerationist narrative.** Gonda's principle of conservative transformation (see [[cultural-memory-and-tradition]]) — that Indian religious forms evolve through cumulative accretion rather than corruption — is directly opposed to the German Indological assumption that accreted texts are degraded texts. Where Gonda sees stratification as a mode of living tradition, Holtzmann saw it as evidence of Brahmanical degeneration.

**J.Z. Smith's parallel critique.** Smith's argument that scholarly comparisons are constructions rather than discoveries (see [[comparative-religion-methodology]]) parallels the Nay Science's argument that Indological reconstructions are projections rather than recoveries. Both challenge the naturalism of academic method — the assumption that the scholar's categories correspond to structures in the data.

**The Mahābhārata as philosophical whole.** The claim that the *Mahābhārata* must be read as a coherent philosophical text rather than a fragmented historical artifact connects to the treatment of dharma in [[dharma]]: the epic's narrative complexity — the simultaneous validity of competing dharmas — is precisely what the historical-critical method cannot accommodate.
