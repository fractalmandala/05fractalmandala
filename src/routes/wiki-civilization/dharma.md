title: dharma

Dharma is the most elastic and load-bearing concept in the Indian intellectual tradition — semantically, it covers cosmic order, moral duty, legal norm, religious teaching, and the nature of things, all under a single term whose root (*dhṛ*, "to hold or sustain") points toward what keeps reality upright. Its history is not a linear development from simple to complex but a palimpsest: each new deployment of the term adds a layer without erasing the previous ones. The result is a concept that simultaneously holds cosmic, legal, narrative, and political registers, and whose tensions — particularly between universal cosmic dharma and particular situational dharma — have driven some of the most productive debates in Indian thought.

## Sources

### Hiltebeitel & Lariviere (eds.), *Dharma: Its Early History*

This volume conducts what the editors call a "semantic archaeology" of dharma — tracking its meaning across texts and periods rather than fixing on a single definition.

**From ṛta to dharma:** The earliest layer is cosmological. In the Ṛgveda, *ṛta* carries the primary weight of cosmic order. Dharma appears as a derivative concept — the "holding" or "sustaining" aspect of that order as it applies to beings. As ṛta fades from active use after the Ṛgveda (becoming more a technical and philosophical term), dharma expands to fill the space, eventually absorbing the entire cosmological, moral, and legal register that ṛta had occupied.

**Dharma as legal-ritual norm:** By the Dharmasūtras and Manusmṛti, dharma has become primarily a prescriptive category — rules for varna conduct, āśrama stages, expiation, inheritance, ritual procedure. This is dharma as codified norm, administered by Brahmin specialists and enforced through the logic of karmic consequence. The texts are explicitly interested in edge cases and conflicts — when two dharmas clash, which takes precedence?

**Ashoka's public dharma:** The Ashokan inscriptions (3rd century BCE) deploy dharma in a radically different register: as a public moral-political discourse addressed to the entire population, regardless of varna or religious affiliation. Ashoka's dharma is minimal and universalist — non-violence, respect for parents and teachers, generosity, truthfulness. It does not invoke Brahmin authority or Vedic revelation. The editors read this as evidence that dharma's semantic range was always broader than the Brahmanical legal tradition implies.

**Narrative destabilization:** The Mahābhārata is the most important single text for understanding dharma's complexity, because it systematically shows dharma failing — or appearing to fail — at every turn. Yudhiṣṭhira, the dharma-king, loses everything through gambling. Karṇa, the most loyal and generous character, fights on the wrong side and dies unjustly. Draupadī's famous question — "What is dharma, O king?" — remains unanswered. The great teachers of dharma (Bhīṣma, Dharma himself as Yudhiṣṭhira's father) offer contradictory teachings. The editors argue that the epic does not resolve these tensions but rather holds them in productive suspension — narrative dharma cannot be reduced to juridical dharma.

**Tripartite structure:** The volume identifies a persistent three-way tension in Indian jurisprudence: *dharma* (religious law), *artha* (political economy), and *nyāya* (logic/argumentation). These are not three aspects of a single system but three partially autonomous normative orders that periodically conflict. The history of Indian legal thought is partly the history of negotiating their overlaps.
