---
title: time, eternity, and cosmic cycles
id: 18
---

Across Indian philosophy and comparative religion, the most fundamental distinction is not between good and evil or the sacred and the profane, but between time and eternity — between the moving image of eternity (Plato's phrase, adopted by Coomaraswamy) and the nunc stans, the standing now that is eternity itself. Time is becoming; eternity is being. The Indian tradition's remarkable contribution is the insistence that time is not neutral background but the form of ignorance — the mind that identifies with temporal becoming rather than the eternal present is, for that reason, bound. Liberation (mokṣa) is not an event in time but a recognition that one always already participates in the timeless.

## Sources

### Coomaraswamy, *Time and Eternity*

Coomaraswamy's study of how time and eternity are understood across traditions (Vedic, Greek, Christian, Buddhist) converges on a single point: eternity is not endless time but the absence of time — the *nunc stans* (the standing now) that Augustine described and that Meister Eckhart made the center of his mysticism.

**Nunc stans vs. nunc fluens:** Ordinary time is *nunc fluens* — the flowing now, perpetually ceasing to be. Each moment vanishes as the next arrives. Eternity is *nunc stans* — the standing now that does not pass, the present that contains all presents. This is not a duration that goes on indefinitely; it is the cessation of temporal movement altogether.

**Time as becoming/ignorance:** For both Vedānta and Platonism, time is the modality of ignorance — the form that reality takes when it is not fully known. The real (Brahman, the Good, the One) is timeless; what is temporal is real only insofar as it participates in the timeless. This is not nihilism about the temporal world but a hierarchy of reality: the temporal is less real, not unreal.

**Creation as timeless ontological dependence:** The creation of the world is not an event that happened at some past moment; it is an eternal relationship of dependence. The cosmos is always being created — at every moment, its existence depends on the sustaining power of the eternal. This dissolves the "when did the world begin?" question by relocating creation from the temporal to the ontological register.

**Participation doctrine:** Following Plato's *methexis* and its Indian analogues, Coomaraswamy argues that beings participate in the Real — they do not merely resemble it or represent it. The flower participates in Beauty; the just act participates in Justice; the meditating yogi participates in Being. Participation is a stronger claim than resemblance: it means the temporal instance genuinely contains, not merely copies, the eternal prototype.

### Daniélou, *While the Gods Play* (cosmic cycles)

Daniélou's account of the yuga system and Hindu cosmology is presented as the literal metaphysical truth of the Śaiva Siddhānta tradition, not as mythology to be interpreted.

**The four yugas:** Reality moves through four ages of decreasing quality and decreasing lifespan of the world: Kṛta Yuga (Satya Yuga — the golden age, dharma complete), Tretā Yuga (dharma reduced by one quarter), Dvāpara Yuga (dharma half), and Kali Yuga (dharma reduced to one quarter, the age of strife). We are currently in the Kali Yuga, which accounts for the degradation, materialism, and dissolution of social bonds visible in contemporary civilization. A Kali Yuga lasts 432,000 years; the full cycle (mahāyuga) is 4,320,000 years; larger cycles (manvantara, kalpa) extend into astronomical time.

**Dissolution and recreation:** At the end of each great cycle, Śiva's dissolution absorbs the universe back into undifferentiated consciousness. This is not destruction but return — the universe is periodically re-absorbed (pralaya) and then projected again (sṛṣṭi). The cycle has no beginning and no end; it is the rhythm of cosmic existence, which Daniélou identifies with the rhythm of breath and heartbeat at the microcosmic level.

**Lila and the acceptance of impermanence:** The cyclical nature of cosmic time is not tragic but is the expression of lila (play). The universe is created and dissolved for the intrinsic joy of the play, not for a purpose external to it. This reframes cosmic impermanence: not as a problem to be solved but as the nature of the game being played.

### Aurobindo, *The Human Cycle*

Aurobindo's *The Human Cycle* (originally *The Psychology of Social Development*) applies a developmental framework to civilization — not the linear progressivism of 19th-century evolutionism, but a cyclical model in which civilizations move through psychological stages that recapitulate the stages of individual spiritual development.

**Psychological stages of civilization:** Aurobindo identifies five stages through which societies move: (1) **Symbolic** — the most ancient, where every social form is a symbol of cosmic truth; the Vedic age, where the varna system represented cosmic forces rather than economic categories. (2) **Typal** — the warrior-priest-merchant-servant division crystallizes into fixed social types, each embodying a virtue. (3) **Conventional** — the types harden into conventions, hereditary castes; the living connection to cosmic meaning is lost; external form survives without inner content. (4) **Individualistic** — the individual rebels against convention, asserts reason and personal freedom; the Enlightenment and modernity. (5) **Subjective/Spiritual** — the individual turns inward and discovers that pure rationalism cannot satisfy; the turn toward interiority, consciousness, and spiritual development.

**Infrarational/rational/suprarational:** Underneath the five stages runs a deeper typology: infrarational modes of organizing life (instinct, tradition, customary rhythm), rational modes (reason, criticism, scientific analysis), and suprarational modes (spiritual intuition, direct knowing, consciousness that transcends rational categories). Modern civilization has mastered the rational but has suppressed the suprarational — Aurobindo sees the subjective turn as the beginning of the recovery of the suprarational.

**Vedic symbolic age:** The Vedic age is, for Aurobindo, the paradigm of the symbolic stage — a civilization in which every social form (the sacrifice, the varna system, the ashrama stages) was a conscious symbol of cosmic truth. The degeneration from this symbolic fullness is not merely historical decline but a necessary stage in the development of individual consciousness: the conventional age, as oppressive as it is, produces the conditions for individual rebellion and self-discovery that lead eventually to the subjective turn.
