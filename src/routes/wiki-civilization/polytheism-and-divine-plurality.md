Edward Butler's *The Metaphysics of Polytheism in Proclus* is the most rigorous philosophical defense of polytheism in contemporary scholarship — a reconstruction of the late Neoplatonist Proclus' "henadology" (doctrine of the divine unities or henads) that argues the henads are not a concession to popular religion or a redundant structural element of Neoplatonic ontology, but the foundational reality upon which all else depends. Butler's work is relevant to Indian religious thought because it provides a philosophical vocabulary for what Indian polytheism's most sophisticated defenders have always claimed: that the many gods are genuinely many, genuinely real, and their multiplicity is not a defect to be overcome by reducing them to one.

## Sources

### Butler, *The Metaphysics of Polytheism in Proclus*

**The central project:** Butler argues that modern commentators have systematically misread Proclus by treating his henadology (doctrine of divine unities/henads) either as a concession to "vulgar" polytheism or as structurally redundant. Butler's counter-argument: the henads are the foundational reality; the entire metaphysical system is organized around them. The project is to restore what has been suppressed — the rigorous philosophical articulation of genuine polytheism.

**The two registers:** Butler distinguishes between the **ontological** register (Proclus is a monist — all reality depends on a single principle, the One) and the **existential or supra-essential register** (Proclus is a pluralist — the One exists only as the multiplicity of unique, primordial individuals called henads). The Gods are the supra-essential level: they are not beings (onta) in the ontological sense; they precede Being and constitute it through their activity.

**Hyparxis and dunamis:** Butler organizes his analysis around two key concepts. **Hyparxis** (existence): the absolute, non-relational, self-identical existence of each God as an irreducible individual. At this level, the Gods are simply who they are — not defined by their functions, their powers, or their relationships. **Dunamis** (power): the productive capacity through which the Gods generate beings and are integrated into the cosmic hierarchy. Only through dunamis can philosophy speak scientifically about "classes of gods" (demiurgic gods, solar gods, etc.); at the level of hyparxis, each God is incommensurable.

**Polycentric polytheism:** Butler introduces this concept to describe the structure of Proclus' system. Each God is "all in all" — contains the entire universe within its own unique perspective, and every other God is present within it according to its own mode. This means any God can serve as the "center" of the system for a worshipper or a specific theological context. This is not relativism; it is a feature of the system's structure. The shift in perspective does not violate the integrity of other Gods or diminish them.

**The primacy of individuality:** Butler's thesis on individuality: for Proclus, the One is not a totalizing monotheistic Absolute that absorbs all difference but the principle of individuation that is fully and uniquely manifest in each individual God. Monotheism, in this reading, is the philosophical error of collapsing divine individuality into a single totality — which fails to recognize that the One is not one being among others but the ground of each being's irreducible uniqueness.

**Divine names as infrastructure:** Proclus treats divine names not as human labels imposed on neutral divine realities, but as revelations — structural features of Being through which the Gods make themselves known. The proper name of a God (Zeus, Athena, Śiva, Viṣṇu) does not classify; it individuates. Philosophy can speak of "classes of gods" (scientific discourse) but only theology and theurgy can speak of individual Gods by their proper names (intellectual discourse). This distinction maps closely onto the Indian distinction between *nirguṇa Brahman* (Brahman without qualities, accessed by philosophical analysis) and *saguṇa Brahman* or the specific deities (accessed by devotion and ritual).

**Myth as performative residue:** Myth is not philosophy simplified for popular consumption; it is a "performative residue" of divine activity — a living contact with the divine that philosophy can interpret but never fully exhaust. The contradictions in myths about a single God (different stories of Zeus' parentage, different stories of Śiva's origins) are not errors; they reflect the God's manifestation through different powers at different levels of Being. The philosopher's job is to interpret myth, but myth always exceeds the interpretation.

**Relevance to Indian polytheism:** Butler does not address Indian religion directly, but his framework illuminates what sophisticated Hindu theologians have always maintained: the devas are genuinely distinct; Viṣṇu and Śiva are not the same God wearing different costumes; the philosophical tendency to reduce all gods to one (whether through Advaita's *nirguṇa Brahman* or through the Western monotheist's dismissal of "idol worship") misses what polytheism actually claims. Butler provides the philosophical architecture for taking the claim seriously.
