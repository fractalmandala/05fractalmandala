The Three Great Dialogues represent a series of profound intellectual and spiritual exchanges that occurred in Thea, spread over many centuries. These dialogues, each between rishis of two different hominid species, were instrumental in revealing the universal essence of the sanatana system across Thean civilizations. By the culmination of these dialogues, it was understood that all six hominid species followed variants of the sanatana system, leading to the term "sanatana sarvatra" which means "eternal omnipresent."

##### first great dialogue
Between Muni Vatsala of Kadorra (Manushya) and Bhaumayogi of Ormaunk (Vaunaki)
The first of the dialogues took place against a backdrop of growing curiosity and occasional friction between the Kadorran and Vaunaki cultures. The rishis engaged in a deep discussion about the nature of the cosmos, the cycle of life and death, and the essence of divinity. This dialogue illuminated the striking similarities in the core beliefs of the Kadorrans and Vaunakis about the universe's cyclical nature and the concept of an overarching cosmic order. It laid the foundation for a shared understanding and respect between the two species.

##### second great dialogue
Between Sadhu Amloh of Prithviyah (Avinaki) and Sadhu Ushna of Arkindha (Kapusha)
Occurring centuries after the first, this dialogue was initiated to address emerging philosophical and theological questions. The Avinakis and Kapushas explored the concepts of creation, consciousness, and the role of technology and nature in their respective spiritual practices. The dialogue led to a profound realization of the underlying congruence in their beliefs, particularly regarding the sanctity of life and the interdependence between technology, nature, and spirituality. It further solidified the notion of a shared spiritual heritage among the species of Thea.

##### third great dialogue
Between Guru Vidant of Madura (Rourushya) and Bhaumayogi III of Rtam (Homo Mycelis)
In an era marked by significant cosmic exploration and interaction, the final dialogue was pivotal. It delved into themes of resilience, adaptation, and the spiritual connection with ancestral roots and the broader cosmos. This dialogue brought to light the resonant themes of endurance, cosmic harmony, and the ancestral reverence found in both the Homo Mycelis and Rourushya spiritual practices. It underscored the universality of the Sanatana principles across different species and environments.

The cumulative insights from these dialogues led to a galactic epiphany. It was recognized that, despite the diversity in rituals, myths, and cultural expressions, all six hominid species of Thea adhered to variations of the same foundational spiritual system - the sanatana. This revelation was groundbreaking, fostering a sense of unity, mutual respect, and spiritual kinship among the species. It marked the beginning of the sanatana sarvatra system, acknowledging the ubiquitous and integrative nature of these spiritual principles.
