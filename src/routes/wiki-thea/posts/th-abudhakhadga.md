##### description and habitat

The abudha-khadga is a unique omnivorous mammalian species native to the Vaunaki planet. Distinguished by their robust build, the abudha-khadga typically weighs around 350-400 kilograms and reaches up to 2 meters in height. They possess a thick, bristly hide, predominantly brown in color with intermittent patches of grey, providing camouflage in their muddy habitates. Their most notable feature is their elongated snout, equipped with sharp incisors and molars adapted for a diverse diet.

Abudha-khadgas are found in the dense, humid forests of the Vaunaki planet of Ormaunk. They are adept climbers and can be often spotted in the lower canopy levels, foraging for food.

##### behavior

Abudha-khadgas are solitary creatures, coming together only during mating seasons. They are known for their territorial nature, marking their territory with distinct scents. Their vocalizations, a series of deep grunts and high-pitched whistles, are used for both mating calls and warning signals. The abudha-khadga has a unique ability to stand on its hind legs for short periods, which they use to reach higher branches or intimidate potential threats. This bipedal stance, coupled with their formidable size, makes them a fascinating sight in the wild forests of Vaunaki.

As omnivores, their diet is diverse, including fruits, leaves, small mammals, and insects. They have a particular preference for the bolla fruit, a native species to Vaunaki, which contributes to their unique digestive system capable of processing a variety of organic materials. The species has a gestation period of approximately five months, after which typically one offspring is born. The young are cared for by the mother for up to two years, learning essential survival skills before venturing off to establish their own territory.

##### culture and media

In Vaunaki culture, the abudha-khadga is often depicted in traditional art and mythology, symbolizing strength and adaptability. They are revered as a totem of resilience and resourcefulness. Currently, there are no significant threats to their population. The Vaunaki people have a deep respect for the species and ensure sustainable coexistence, with strict guidelines against hunting and habitat destruction.
