This page covers the corpus on Christianity, missions, indigenisation, and the long historical encounter between Hindu civilization and Christian expansion. The central tendency of these works is to argue that Hindu-Christian relations cannot be understood as a neutral "dialogue" alone; they are also shaped by missionary theology, institutional power, colonial protection, and disputes over representation, culture, and conversion.

## Historical Arc

- [History of Hindu Christian Encounters](/wiki-srgsrs/posts/438) is the anchor text and surveys encounters from late antiquity to the late twentieth century.
- The early Syrian Christian presence is treated differently from later Portuguese and missionary Christianity.
- Portuguese and later missionary encounters are presented as qualitatively more aggressive, especially where theological exclusivism linked itself to political or colonial power.
- Later Christian adaptation in India is often described in these books as strategic indigenisation rather than genuine theological reciprocity.

## Key Sub-Themes

- Missionary critique: Christianity is criticized not only for conversion but for recoding Hindu symbols into Christian frameworks.
- Indigenisation and ashramization: Christian adoption of Hindu idioms is treated skeptically in Catholic-ashram debates rather than celebrated as synthesis.
- Christology and church power: Some books move from encounter history into broader critiques of Jesus, papal claims, and Christian doctrinal authority.
- Public controversy: Christian missionary resistance to scrutiny also links this page with [[secularism-free-expression-and-political-language]].
