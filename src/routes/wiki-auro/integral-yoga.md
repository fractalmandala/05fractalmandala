Integral Yoga is Sri Aurobindo's attempt to synthesize the major paths of yoga into a discipline aimed not only at liberation but at the transformation of the whole being. It includes works, knowledge, devotion, psychic opening, descent of higher consciousness, and the eventual transformation of mind, life, and body. This topic is the practical counterpart to [[metaphysics-and-spiritual-evolution]] and receives scriptural preparation from [[gita-and-karmayoga]] and [[upanishads-and-vedanta]].

## Main Themes

- Yoga as transformation of life rather than escape from existence.
- Surrender, equality, aspiration, rejection, and consecration as foundational disciplines.
- Experiences and realizations treated as stages, not ends in themselves.
- The later letters provide case-based guidance on obstacles, psychology, and embodied change.

## Source Summaries

### `23-24TheSynthesisofYoga.pdf`

This is Sri Aurobindo's principal systematic work on yoga and the clearest formal statement of Integral Yoga. It opens from the proposition that "all life is yoga" and then develops the synthesis through the yoga of works, the yoga of knowledge, devotion, and related disciplines. Although left incomplete, it lays out the architecture of a practice that seeks transformation rather than exclusive liberation.

### `28LettersOnYoga-I.pdf`

The first volume of the letters focuses on the philosophical and psychological foundations of Integral Yoga. Its sections on the Divine, the planes of consciousness, the evolutionary process, and broader problems of religion and philosophy make it a bridge between practice and doctrine. It is especially useful for clarifying the technical vocabulary that later volumes presuppose.

### `29LettersOnYoga-II.pdf`

This volume turns to actual practice: the path, the goal, requisites such as sincerity and surrender, the foundation of peace and equality, and the relation between effort and divine response. It is less a formal treatise than a practical manual built from correspondence, which makes it valuable for understanding how Sri Aurobindo adapted general principles to individual conditions.

### `30LettersOnYoga-III.pdf`

The third volume addresses experiences and realizations: inner senses, inner consciousness, cosmic consciousness, and the major spiritual openings that can occur in practice. Its importance lies in its cautionary and discriminating tone, since Sri Aurobindo repeatedly distinguishes between experiences that are genuine, partial, mixed, premature, or misleading.

### `31LettersOnYoga-IV.pdf`

The final volume centers on transformation of human nature at the mental, vital, physical, subconscient, and inconscient levels. It also discusses the resistances, difficulties, and adverse movements that practitioners encounter. This makes it the most detailed practical source in the corpus for the arduous side of embodied spiritual change.
