Ṛta and Dharma

Ṛta is the oldest word in the Vedic vocabulary for cosmic order — the principle that the sun rises, the seasons turn, and the rains come in their time. It is not a law imposed from outside but the inherent rhythm of existence. Dharma, the term that later supersedes it, carries this meaning forward while adding moral and social weight: the right action, the right role, the right relationship.

The Vedic Conception

In the Rigveda, Ṛta appears in over three hundred hymns. Varuna and Mitra are its guardians — cosmic sovereigns who maintain the order of the universe and punish its violators. The opposite of Ṛta is anṛta, untruth, disorder, the lie that disturbs the cosmic fabric.

The paths of Ṛta are beautiful and thornless; on that path, free from obstructions, let us travel. — Rigveda 1.41.4

The term also carries an astronomical meaning. The regularity of the heavens — the nakṣatras, the solar and lunar cycles — was itself Ṛta made visible. The Vedic ṛṣi saw no separation between cosmic, moral, and ritual order: the sacrifice was a re-enactment of the cosmic ordering principle.

The Transition to Dharma

By the time of the Upaniṣads and the Mahābhārata, Dharma has largely absorbed and expanded Ṛta. Where Ṛta described the impersonal cosmic order, Dharma becomes simultaneously the universal law, the social code, and the individual duty — all three simultaneously active in any given act. This is the characteristic compression of Indian thought: the cosmic, the social, and the personal are not separate domains.

Dharma as Sustainer

The Sanskrit root dhṛ — to hold, to sustain — is embedded in the word itself. Dharma is not a constraint placed upon life but the very structure that makes life possible. This is why the Mahābhārata's most famous line — dharmo rakṣati rakṣitaḥ, dharma protects those who protect it — is not a moral exhortation but a statement of cosmic fact.

The implications for political philosophy are considerable. A king who violates dharma does not merely do wrong; he unravels the structure that sustains his own kingdom. The collapse is not punishment but consequence — the natural result of disorder introduced into the cosmic fabric.
